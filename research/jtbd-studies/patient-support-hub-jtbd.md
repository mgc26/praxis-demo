# Top Jobs-to-Be-Done on US Manufacturer Patient Support Hub Lines for Dravet and DEE and Essential Tremor Therapies

## Scope and what a manufacturer hub does in practice

A manufacturer “hub” (patient support hub line) exists because getting on and staying on a specialty therapy is often a multi-party logistics problem: payer rules (benefit verification, prior authorization, step edits), specialty pharmacy routing, financial support, required monitoring or REMS steps, and ongoing adherence and troubleshooting. An industry overview describes hubs as a manufacturer’s single point of contact with patients using the therapy, offering services that commonly include benefits investigation, prior authorization processing, drug delivery and administration help, financial and copay support, patient education, REMS support, data reporting, and bridge or quick-start supplies and prescription triage. citeturn20view0

This model shows up strongly in rare pediatric epilepsy (DEE and Dravet) because the clinical stakes are high and the caregiver is the operational center of gravity. Dravet begins in infancy or early childhood, is often severe, and is associated with refractory seizures and significant developmental impacts. citeturn8search0turn13view0turn8search3 Caregivers are frequently managing multiple antiseizure medications, rescue meds, school coordination, nocturnal monitoring, and repeated healthcare system navigation. citeturn22view2turn22view1

Essential tremor is not rare; it is described as the most common movement disorder, and it can cause functional disability (writing, eating, drinking) and social embarrassment. citeturn8search29turn15view0turn15view1 Even so, newer prescription-based treatments (notably certain neuromodulation devices) can behave like “specialty” access problems because coverage is inconsistent and documentation requirements can be strict. For example, one prescription device manufacturer states its team supports patients “from submitting reimbursement to using therapy” and provides customer care and training. citeturn28view0turn24view2 Meanwhile, Medicare coverage for an external upper limb tremor stimulator includes specific initial criteria and a required re-evaluation between day 60 and day 91 for continued coverage beyond three months, which creates an administrative cliff inside the first 90 days. citeturn29view0turn27view2 Commercial coverage can vary sharply, with multiple payer medical policies classifying transcutaneous afferent patterned stimulation (TAPS) as investigational for essential tremor. citeturn28view1turn27view4turn27view1

## Regulatory constraints that shape hub-line work

**FDA communications boundaries (what agents can say).** Hub agents sit in the blast radius of FDA “truthful and non-misleading” expectations for product communications and the “fair balance” principle in prescription drug advertising. citeturn7search0turn7search9 In practice, hubs typically separate (1) access and administrative support, (2) medical information handling of unsolicited clinical questions, and (3) pharmacovigilance intake. FDA guidance on responding to unsolicited requests about unapproved uses is a key constraint: you can respond, but you must do it in a controlled, non-promotional way, typically via medical information rather than frontline access staff. citeturn1search5

**Pharmacovigilance and product complaint intake.** For drugs, manufacturers (and their contracted vendors) must have a postmarketing safety reporting process; serious and unexpected adverse drug experiences require expedited reporting (commonly within 15 calendar days from initial receipt by the applicant). citeturn9search17 A core operational constraint is that the “clock” effectively starts when any company personnel receive the minimum information for a valid report, and minimum criteria include: identifiable patient, identifiable reporter, suspect product, and adverse event/reaction. citeturn11view6turn9search5turn9search17 For essential tremor device therapies, medical device reporting obligations differ and are governed by 21 CFR Part 803 (generally 30-day reporting once the manufacturer becomes aware of a reportable event). citeturn7search2turn7search6

**HIPAA and health data privacy.** Many hub workflows rely on documented authorizations and data-sharing permissions because clinical offices, specialty pharmacies, and insurers are frequently HIPAA covered entities, and disclosures for “marketing” generally require authorization. citeturn30search2turn30search4 In a real-world example of a patient support start form, the prescriber attests that the patient’s authorization has been obtained for disclosures to support benefits investigation, pharmacy routing, signatures and consents, and patient assistance program eligibility; the patient authorization section also explicitly allows disclosure of diagnoses and insurance benefit details to the manufacturer and its vendors for program operation and coverage support. citeturn17view1turn17view0 The same form acknowledges caregiver signature on behalf of the patient (with authorization) and notes that some disclosures may no longer be protected by HIPAA once in the manufacturer ecosystem, shifting the burden to privacy practices and state privacy laws. citeturn17view0

Also relevant is whether a hub vendor is acting as a HIPAA business associate, which is defined as an entity performing functions involving PHI on behalf of a covered entity. citeturn7search11turn7search7

**Financial assistance constraints (OIG and anti-kickback).** Manufacturer copay assistance and free-drug programs have to be designed to avoid improper inducements, especially involving federal healthcare program beneficiaries. A federal watchdog report on copay coupons highlights broad concerns that coupons can increase costs and create steering effects, and the industry norm is to exclude federal program beneficiaries from typical commercial copay cards. citeturn1search8 In patient-facing program terms, exclusions and restrictions are explicit: for example, a copay savings program describes ineligibility for Medicare/Medicaid/VA-DoD and prohibits submission of reimbursement claims to government programs. citeturn12view0 Similar terms and program constraints appear in rare epilepsy support programs. citeturn11view5turn12view0

**State-level constraints (practical impact on calls).**
- **Call recording consent:** Consent rules vary by state (one-party vs all-party). A national 50-state survey summarizes these consent requirements, which matters because many hubs record calls for QA and compliance. citeturn5search0  
- **Nursing licensure and telehealth-by-phone:** If a hub uses nurse navigators who provide nursing services, telehealth nursing is generally treated as nursing practice regulated by boards of nursing. citeturn5search11turn5search3 Multi-state practice may be facilitated by licensure compacts, but requirements vary. citeturn5search19turn5search7  
- **State privacy laws beyond HIPAA:** Washington’s My Health My Data Act is an example of broad “consumer health data” regulation that applies outside HIPAA contexts and restricts sale of consumer health data without specific authorization, effective for many provisions starting March 31, 2024. citeturn5search1turn5search9

**Outbound contact constraints (TCPA and related rules).** While your question focuses on inbound calls, best-in-class hubs also do outbound “save” calls, refill reminders, and check-ins. A major constraint is the Telephone Consumer Protection Act, which restricts autodialed or prerecorded calls/texts without prior express consent (with exceptions). citeturn30search1turn30search0 The FTC’s Telemarketing Sales Rule includes healthcare-related carve-outs in certain contexts (for example, prerecorded healthcare messages made by or on behalf of a covered entity or its business associate). citeturn30search3 HIPAA also has specific guidance that refill reminders and certain treatment-related communications are excluded from the definition of “marketing” under defined conditions. citeturn30search11

## Top jobs-to-be-done in inbound hub calls

The “top 10” below are phrased as jobs (what the caller is trying to accomplish), not as hub functions. In Dravet/DEE, the caller is frequently a parent or legal guardian operating under extreme time pressure and sleep deprivation; in essential tremor, the caller is often the patient or spouse/partner navigating embarrassment, functional loss, and payer friction. citeturn13view0turn22view1turn15view1

### Job: Find out if the therapy is covered and what the patient will actually pay

**Triggering event:** A new prescription is written, the pharmacy says “needs PA,” the patient sees a shockingly high quoted copay, or the prescriber’s office tells the family “call the program for coverage support.” citeturn20view0turn12view1

**Emotional state:** In Dravet/DEE: urgency, fear, anger at delays, and cognitive overload (many caregivers are already anxious and sleep-deprived). citeturn13view0turn22view1 In essential tremor: frustration, resignation, and embarrassment if the condition is limiting daily tasks. citeturn15view1turn15view0

**“Done well” looks like:** The caller gets a clear answer on coverage status, expected out-of-pocket cost range, what’s blocking access (PA, step therapy, routing), and an explicit next-step timeline. They do not have to call multiple parties to understand what’s happening.

**Regulatory constraints:** Agents must avoid making coverage promises (“guarantees”) and must treat payer decisions as payer-controlled; manufacturers’ own patient support pages often caveat that support does not guarantee coverage outcomes. citeturn12view0turn12view1 If PHI is exchanged, it must be under appropriate authorization and minimum-necessary discipline where applicable. citeturn17view0turn7search11

**How hubs handle today:** Human-staffed hubs route this to benefits verification (BV) specialists and case managers. ConnectiveRx positions e-services as a way to navigate lengthy PA and benefit verification and speed access. citeturn21view2 Lash Group case-study materials describe completing benefit verifications, reporting coverage back to providers, and triaging prescriptions to appropriate specialty pharmacies. citeturn11view0 EVERSANA similarly describes hub programs and e-services to help patients navigate onboarding and access barriers. citeturn21view1turn6search7

### Job: Get enrolled and give the hub permission to act

**Triggering event:** The hub has no enrollment on file, the caregiver is told a signed consent is required, or the specialty pharmacy will not ship without documentation.

**Emotional state:** In Dravet/DEE: “I do not have time for forms,” plus fear of missing a treatment window and exhaustion. citeturn13view0turn11view4 In essential tremor: impatience and worry about being unable to do basic tasks.

**“Done well” looks like:** The hub makes the enrollment process feel simple: minimal steps, clear explanation of what the consent enables, and options to complete via phone, electronic signature, fax, or secure upload. The caregiver clearly understands what the hub can and cannot do.

**Regulatory constraints:** Enrollment processes are constrained by HIPAA permissions and by the “marketing” definition and its exceptions; treatment and access support can be permissible, but marketing communications generally require authorization when covered entities are involved. citeturn30search2turn17view0 State privacy laws can impose additional requirements or longer retention rules, and consent for call recording may be state-dependent. citeturn5search0turn5search1

**How hubs handle today:** Start forms and consent workflows are standardized. For example, an epilepsy patient support program start form includes provider authorization for disclosure to insurers, pharmacies, and the manufacturer and a separate HIPAA patient authorization describing permitted uses such as benefits investigation, reimbursement coordination, and contact by phone/SMS. citeturn17view1turn17view0 ConnectiveRx promotes e-consent and electronic workflow integration to connect patients to hub services through the prescriber’s e-Rx workflow. citeturn21view2

### Job: Resolve prior authorization, denial, or appeal status

**Triggering event:** A denial letter arrives, the prescriber’s office says “PA is required,” the payer requests more clinical documentation, or the patient is bouncing between pharmacies due to routing rules.

**Emotional state:** Anger, panic, and distrust of “the system” are common, especially when the caller has already made multiple calls and still has no medication. In Dravet/DEE, this often stacks on top of chronic traumatic stress and caregiver burden. citeturn22view1turn11view4

**“Done well” looks like:** The hub can explain, in plain language, what the payer needs and what the prescriber must submit, and can keep the caregiver informed without making them chase status. “Done” means either approval, a clear next-best option (appeal, alternate coverage route), or a realistic stop decision.

**Regulatory constraints:** Information must be accurate and non-misleading; agents can support PA mechanics but should not “practice medicine” or override prescriber judgment. citeturn12view0turn12view1 Documentation exchange must occur under proper authorization and secure channels. citeturn17view0turn7search11

**How hubs handle today:** This is core hub work. In a Dravet epilepsy support program description, the hub determines benefits and initiates PA support, provides payer-specific PA forms, and can assist with appeal documentation as needed. citeturn11view2 Manufacturer patient support pages also explicitly list prior authorization and appeals as typical support offerings. citeturn12view1 Vendor models rely on case managers to coordinate between patient, HCP, insurer, and specialty pharmacy. citeturn21view3

### Job: Get the prescription to the right dispensing channel and get shipment scheduled

**Triggering event:** The patient is told “you must use a different specialty pharmacy,” the specialty pharmacy cannot reach the caregiver, shipment is delayed, cold-chain timing is complicated, or a limited distribution network restricts access.

**Emotional state:** Time pressure and rage at “administrative delays.” In Dravet/DEE, delays feel medically unsafe because the condition is severe and caregivers may already be operating on limited sleep and high anxiety. citeturn13view0turn8search0

**“Done well” looks like:** The caregiver knows which pharmacy will dispense, when the first shipment will arrive, what needs to happen for refill cadence, and who owns which step. Nothing falls through handoffs.

**Regulatory constraints:** State e-prescribing rules vary and can affect how prescriptions and support documentation are transmitted. citeturn11view2turn23view0 Agents must protect PHI and coordinate only under authorization. citeturn17view0

**How hubs handle today:** A hub process document for an epilepsy therapy describes the hub determining the payer’s preferred specialty pharmacy, notifying the prescriber with BV results, and relying on the dispensing specialty pharmacy to contact the patient to arrange shipment. citeturn11view2 Lash Group case-study materials describe prescription triage to appropriate specialty pharmacies and highlight that redundant authorizations and failure to reach the patient can create fulfillment delays that hubs attempt to identify and remove. citeturn11view0

### Job: Lower out-of-pocket cost or find a viable financial pathway

**Triggering event:** Copay or coinsurance is unaffordable, the family hits a deductible, an accumulator/maximizer program blocks expected copay relief, or the patient is uninsured/underinsured.

**Emotional state:** Shame, anxiety, and sometimes desperation. In Dravet/DEE, it often presents as “I will do anything but I cannot pay this,” combined with exhaustion. citeturn11view4turn13view0

**“Done well” looks like:** The hub provides an actionable plan: copay savings if eligible, bridge or quick start if available, patient assistance program screening, and referrals to independent foundations when appropriate. The caller understands eligibility rules and what documentation is needed.

**Regulatory constraints:** Copay programs generally exclude federal program beneficiaries and must follow anti-kickback and related fraud-and-abuse constraints. citeturn1search8turn12view0 If a covered entity is involved, “marketing” communications and remuneration arrangements have HIPAA relevance. citeturn30search2turn17view0

**How hubs handle today:** Rare epilepsy programs show financial support as a first-class feature, including support not only for drug cost but also for required monitoring costs. A Dravet therapy financial support sheet describes copay assistance, uninsured access support, and an echocardiogram copay support program. citeturn11view5turn10view5 Another program describes copay savings, a patient assistance program for uninsured or non-covered patients, and a “Quick Start” option providing up to 60 days of therapy while insurance coverage is pending. citeturn12view0turn17view1 A hub vendor report describes copay programs integrated into hubs and tracked within the hub workflow for insight and reduced administrative burden. citeturn11view1

### Job: Learn how to start safely, administer correctly, and reduce practical errors

**Triggering event:** First fill arrives and the caregiver is uncertain about dosing, device use, storage, titration, or what to expect in early weeks. For device therapies, the patient needs setup and training.

**Emotional state:** Nervousness and fear of making a mistake. In Dravet/DEE, this is amplified because caregivers often manage complex regimens and multiple comorbid needs. citeturn22view2turn8search3 In essential tremor, embarrassment and fear of visible “failure” using the device in public can be present. citeturn15view1

**“Done well” looks like:** Clear, scripted guidance consistent with labeling, teach-back behavior (“tell me how you’ll do it”), and easy escalation to a nurse educator for practical coaching. For devices, “done” includes the patient being trained to actually use it correctly.

**Regulatory constraints:** Non-clinician agents must not give individualized medical advice; they can provide approved instructions for use, escalate to nurse navigators, and direct medical questions back to prescribers. Programs explicitly state that treatment decisions rest with the treating clinician. citeturn12view0turn12view1 State nursing licensure constraints apply if nurse educators deliver nursing services across state lines. citeturn5search11turn5search19

**How hubs handle today:** Hubs commonly offer education and nurse support. A nurse navigator program describes working with the patient and caregiver on goals, staying on track, and providing information on administration, dosing and titration, and side effect management, plus practical life-change navigation. citeturn12view0 Device manufacturers describe shipping the device and providing patient training. citeturn24view2turn28view0

### Job: Coordinate required monitoring and REMS or coverage re-evaluations

**Triggering event:** The caregiver is told the therapy requires baseline or periodic tests; the payer requires re-evaluation documentation; a REMS enrollment step is missing; or the pharmacy cannot dispense until criteria are met.

**Emotional state:** Overwhelm and annoyance at “extra hoops,” mixed with safety-driven fear. In Dravet/DEE, caregivers can interpret delays as putting their child at risk. citeturn8search0turn13view0

**“Done well” looks like:** The hub tells the caller exactly what is required, by whom, and by when; provides reminders; and prevents missed milestones that stop dispensing or continuation.

**Regulatory constraints:** REMS elements can impose specific enrollment and certification requirements that hubs must support without improvisation. citeturn20view0turn19view0 For device continuation coverage, Medicare and other payers can require specific documentation inside defined time windows. citeturn29view0turn27view2

**How hubs handle today:** In a Dravet therapy label, echocardiograms are required before treatment, every 6 months during treatment, and once 3 to 6 months after stopping; the therapy is available only through a restricted REMS program with prescriber, patient, and pharmacy enrollment/certification requirements. citeturn19view0turn18view0 That same ecosystem often pairs REMS requirements with financial support for required monitoring, including echocardiogram copay support. citeturn11view5 For essential tremor device therapy under Medicare, continued coverage beyond three months requires a re-evaluation between day 60 and day 91 and documented benefit plus adherence. citeturn29view0turn27view2

### Job: Troubleshoot early side effects or safety concerns and decide who to call next

**Triggering event:** The child becomes sleepier, loses appetite, or has behavioral changes; caregivers worry the therapy is “making things worse”; an adult essential tremor patient experiences skin irritation or discomfort using a device; or labs come back abnormal.

**Emotional state:** Fear and “hypervigilance.” In Dravet/DEE, caregiver anxiety and poor sleep are common and can heighten perceived threat from any change. citeturn13view0turn11view4

**“Done well” looks like:** The hub takes the concern seriously, screens for urgency (emergency vs non-urgent), documents appropriately, provides only labeling-consistent information, and routes quickly to the right destination: prescriber, nurse navigator, medical information, or safety reporting.

**Regulatory constraints:** This is where FDA promotions, medical advice limits, and pharmacovigilance collide. Side-effect discussions can trigger adverse event reporting requirements, and agents must capture minimum required elements. citeturn11view6turn9search5 Agents cannot give individualized medical recommendations unless appropriately credentialed and operating within licensure constraints. citeturn12view0turn5search11

**How hubs handle today:** Programs often provide nurse navigator support that explicitly includes considerations for managing side effects, while still stating treatment decisions rest with the clinician. citeturn12view0turn12view1 Certain labels highlight common adverse reactions that may prompt early calls and discontinuation pressure, including decreased appetite/weight and somnolence. citeturn19view1 A hub vendor describes that case managers are often the first to learn about side effects and are obligated to communicate safety information, reflecting the practical integration of access teams with pharmacovigilance pathways. citeturn21view3

### Job: Report an adverse event or product complaint and feel confident it was handled

**Triggering event:** The caller says “I think this caused X,” “this is a bad reaction,” “the product looks wrong,” “the shipment arrived warm,” or “the device malfunctioned.”

**Emotional state:** Anxiety and a desire for validation that the report matters.

**“Done well” looks like:** The caller feels heard; the agent captures the right information efficiently; and the caller is told what will happen next (follow-up, who to contact for urgent medical care, replacement logistics if applicable).

**Regulatory constraints:** Postmarketing AE reporting for drugs has defined expectations and inspection scrutiny focused on the four data elements (patient, reporter, suspect product, event). citeturn9search5turn11view6 Serious and unexpected events can require 15-day reporting. citeturn9search17 For devices, MDR requirements follow different reporting mechanics and timelines. citeturn7search2turn7search6

**How hubs handle today:** Vendors commonly operationalize this via dedicated intake workflows and system integration into safety and quality systems. EVERSANA explicitly lists “adverse event and product complaint intake” with documentation and triage integrated into safety and quality systems. citeturn21view0

### Job: Replace therapy, fix dispensing issues, or resolve a “break in therapy” risk

**Triggering event:** The caregiver missed a refill, the patient is traveling, medication was lost or contaminated, the device or accessory is missing, or a shipment was delayed or damaged.

**Emotional state:** Panic when a break in therapy seems imminent, especially in severe epilepsy.

**“Done well” looks like:** The hub offers a concrete path to prevent interruption: coordinates with the specialty pharmacy, clarifies whether a replacement or bridge is available, and gives a precise timeline.

**Regulatory constraints:** Any replacement, bridge, or free-good pathway must follow program rules and fraud-and-abuse constraints; for device accessories, coverage and proof-of-delivery rules can matter. citeturn29view0turn12view0 Privacy and consent still apply during coordination. citeturn17view0

**How hubs handle today:** Industry descriptions include bridge supply, quick-start programs, and prescription triage as standard hub services. citeturn20view0 A specialty pharmacy supporting patient support programs lists “QuickStart,” “Bridge,” and “Replacement” among free-goods program types it supports, reflecting how continuity-of-therapy is operationalized. citeturn23view0

### Job: Get caregiver support, validation, and resources that reduce burnout rather than adding tasks

**Triggering event:** The caregiver hits a breaking point, feels isolated, can’t sleep, is overwhelmed by the child’s medical needs, or has been navigating repeated denials and paperwork. Sometimes the “official reason” for the call is administrative, but the emotional need surfaces quickly.

**Emotional state:** Burnout, grief, irritability, guilt, and chronic anxiety. In Dravet caregivers, abnormal sleep quality and clinically relevant anxiety and depressive symptoms are common in published cohorts. citeturn13view0turn11view4

**“Done well” looks like:** The caller feels respected, not rushed; the hub lowers the cognitive load (summaries, written follow-up, fewer handoffs); and the hub offers credible caregiver resources, including peer support and crisis-aware navigation.

**Regulatory constraints:** Agents must remain within scope; they can provide emotional support and resource referrals but must avoid practicing psychotherapy. Data-sharing with third-party resources requires permission; many patient support forms explicitly contemplate sharing with advocacy organizations and similar programs. citeturn17view0turn30search2

**How hubs handle today:** Best-in-class in rare pediatric epilepsy often includes tight linkage to caregiver-specific programs. The Dravet Syndrome Foundation describes caregiver burden as mentally and physically exhausting and offers structured “Caregiver Connect” modules focused on caregiver burnout, emotional communication, and grief, developed with pediatric traumatic stress experts and supported by an educational grant. citeturn22view1turn11view4 A separate DSF post highlights a free caregiver-oriented platform developed with UCB, designed to organize and manage care information in one place, explicitly acknowledging how challenging rare-disease caregiving is and aiming to increase caregiver control. citeturn22view0 Hub vendors also describe case managers as providing emotional support alongside administrative navigation. citeturn21view3

## Caregiver experience and burnout in pediatric DEE and Dravet calls

The caregiver experience in Dravet and other DEEs is qualitatively different from most adult specialty categories. The disease begins early, seizures can be frequent and prolonged, and mortality risk includes SUDEP and status epilepticus, which makes caregivers feel that every delay and every adverse effect could have catastrophic consequences. citeturn13view0turn8search0turn8search4 The result is a caregiver population showing measurable sleep and mental health strain: in a multicenter study of Dravet caregivers, about 77% had abnormal sleep quality (PSQI), and substantial proportions screened above cutoffs for anxiety and depressive symptoms; caregiver burden scores reflected moderate burden in many participants. citeturn13view0

Burnout manifests on hub calls in ways that are operationally predictable:
- **Compressed cognition:** caregivers ask the same thing repeatedly, lose track of dates and steps, and need the agent to restate a plan in simpler form, consistent with sleep impairment and high stress burden. citeturn13view0turn11view4
- **Anger without “rudeness intent”:** a caregiver may sound hostile because they are exhausted and have been blocked by payer friction, not because of the agent. DSF caregiver resources explicitly acknowledge frustration and stress reactions and normalize asking for help when feelings persist or worsen. citeturn11view4turn22view1
- **Hypervigilance and over-triage:** caregivers treat ambiguous symptoms as emergencies, consistent with the genuine risk profile and chronic traumatic stress framing used in caregiver burnout education materials. citeturn22view1turn8search0

What best-in-class programs do about it is not mystical; it is operational empathy coupled with load reduction:

1) **They remove steps, not just “provide information.”** The best caregiver support makes the caregiver do less administrative work by taking ownership of the messy parts (BV, PA packets, pharmacy triage, status updates). Case manager models emphasize reducing administrative burden on both patients and HCP offices. citeturn21view3turn11view0

2) **They explicitly design caregiver-facing mental health support as education and referral, not therapy.** DSF’s Caregiver Connect is a concrete example: modular content, tip sheets, and normalization of stress responses, developed with pediatric traumatic stress experts and intended to fit into chaotic caregiver schedules. citeturn22view1turn11view4

3) **They provide nurse navigation that addresses “practical challenges” over time, including coverage transitions.** A nurse navigator program description includes help with motivation, progress check-ins, practical challenges, life changes, and insurance changes, which are common destabilizers for continuity. citeturn12view0

4) **They use caregiver tools that centralize care information.** A caregiver platform aimed at organizing and sharing care-related information is explicitly framed as making caregivers feel more in control. citeturn22view0

## Why patients abandon specialty therapies within the first 90 days and what phone agents can fix

First, define the “90-day abandonment” problem clearly. One IQVIA report in oncology defines abandonment as failing to fill therapy within 90 days of gaining payer approval. citeturn34view1 That definition is directly relevant to hub operations because “approval” does not equal “medication in hand,” and the hub’s job is to close that gap.

Across specialty categories, the dominant early abandonment drivers are not subtle:

**Out-of-pocket cost and benefit design shock.** IQVIA Institute work notes abandonment under 5% when out-of-pocket cost is $0, rising sharply with higher cost sharing (45% when cost exceeds $125, 60% when cost exceeds $500). citeturn32search1 In the oncology report example, abandonment rates can reach 67% to 81% when costs exceed $250 (varying by pharmacy type and payer). citeturn34view1 A systematic review finds increasing cost sharing above $100 associated with up to 75% abandonment for certain specialty drugs, and it concludes that lower cost sharing via patient support programs is associated with higher adherence and lower discontinuation. citeturn32search4

**Payer controls and claim rejections that are not resolved quickly.** IQVIA describes rising prescription claim rejection rates and reports that close to a third of commercially insured patients rejected at first fill attempt remained unable to access their therapy of choice 30 days later. citeturn34view0turn33view0 This is exactly the period when a hub’s “save” behaviors matter.

**Delays caused by channel complexity and handoffs.** Limited distribution networks and specialty routing create delays, and a study on limited distribution networks emphasizes that such networks can influence patient outcomes through delays and access dynamics. citeturn3search14turn20view0 Lash Group materials provide a concrete example of delay mechanisms: redundant benefit verification, extra authorizations to ship, and inability to reach the patient delaying fulfillment. citeturn11view0

**Monitoring burden and “program friction.”** Some therapies require substantial monitoring and enrollment steps. ### Which early abandonment reasons are addressable by a phone-based support agent?

A phone-based agent can materially reduce abandonment when the cause is administrative, financial, or behavioral, and cannot when the cause is fundamentally clinical or payer-policy hard stop.

**High addressability by phone:**
- Benefits verification clarity, pharmacy routing, and step-by-step navigation. citeturn20view0turn11view2  
- PA packet coordination and appeal documentation scaffolding, including payer-specific forms and follow-up. citeturn11view2turn12view1  
- Copay and PAP pathway matching, including screening, documentation checklists, and bridging while coverage is pending (where program rules allow). citeturn12view0turn11view5turn17view1  
- Monitoring logistics: reminders, scheduling nudges, explaining why tests are required, and preventing a missed REMS or re-eval milestone. citeturn19view0turn29view0  
- Early adherence support: reinforcing what “success in the first month” looks like, addressing practical barriers, and coordinating with nurse navigators for side effect concerns and education. citeturn12view0turn21view3turn34view0  
- Caregiver and emotional load reduction through empathetic support and credible referrals, especially in pediatric rare epilepsy. citeturn22view1turn11view4

**Low addressability by phone:**
- A payer that will not cover the therapy under plan rules, absent successful appeal or clinical pathway change.
- Severe adverse reactions or clinical non-response that require prescriber-led treatment changes.
- Structural barriers outside the hub’s control (housing instability, inability to attend appointments, lack of caregiver capacity without social services involvement).

## How best-in-class hubs operationalize these jobs today

Human-staffed hub operations have converged on a few patterns that are consistently visible across vendor and manufacturer examples:

**Case management as the “integration layer.”** The vendor framing is that a single case manager bridges patient, HCP, insurer, and specialty pharmacy and reduces delays and abandonment risk, with explicit emphasis on empathy plus insurance-process expertise. citeturn21view3turn21view2 The Lash Group case example shows this in practice by analyzing delay root causes across hub-pharmacy handoffs and changing process steps (removing extra patient authorizations, importing sub-statuses into CRM) to speed to fulfillment. citeturn11view0

**Measuring outcomes, not just call-center SLAs.** IQVIA explicitly argues that patient services measurement has historically over-weighted vendor SLAs (call volume, answer rates) instead of true performance indicators tied to whether patients start and stay on therapy. citeturn34view2turn33view2 Their 2026 perspective also pushes measurement toward early access milestones (time to BV/PA completion, time to first fill) rather than internal efficiency vanity metrics. citeturn33view0turn34view0

**Financial support designed as a workflow, not a brochure.** Hubs often integrate copay and assistance workflows into the case record and provider-facing portals to reduce friction and increase confidence that administrative burdens are reduced. citeturn11view1turn21view3

**Embedded nurse navigation for “practical challenges,” not just clinical trivia.** Nurse navigator programs in epilepsy explicitly include goal setting, adherence motivation, education on administration/titration, side effect considerations, and insurance transition support. citeturn12view0 The rare epilepsy pattern also includes nurse educator access linked directly to financial support and monitoring support (for example, a “clinical nurse educator” tied to program questions). citeturn11view5

**Caregiver-first design in pediatric rare epilepsy.** Best-in-class support acknowledges that the “patient” interacting with the hub is frequently the caregiver. Programs that explicitly target caregiver burden, isolation, frustration, and traumatic stress exist and are built as structured modules and tip sheets that can be used asynchronously. citeturn22view1turn11view4 Caregiver tooling that centralizes care coordination is positioned as a way to restore control and reduce chaos. citeturn22view0
